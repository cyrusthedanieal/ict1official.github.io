Bicol's Food

Bicol Express

This creamy and savory meal often contains pork, shrimp paste, tomatoes, onions, green chile, and coconut milk. It also has the appropriate amount of heat to give it a kick. This dish tastes best when paired with plain rice and is available in almost every karinderya and restaurant in Bicol. But if you're feeling particularly daring, Bicol Express can also be combined with pasta sauce.

Laing

Laing, one of the most well-known Bicolano dishes, is a coconut-milk-based meal flavored with siling labuyo, tanglad (lemongrass), bagoong, and minced gabi or taro leaves (shrimp paste).

Kinalas

You shouldn't miss eating this locally produced Naga noodle dish while visiting Bicol. It looks similar to beef mami, a meal that is popular among Filipinos.

Pinangat

Pork/fish is wrapped in gabi and banana or coconut leaves to create a meat-and-vegetable "pouch" that is slowly cooked in coconut milk, peppers, and spices.

Kinunot

- Flaked Padi (stingray), malunggay (moringa) leaves, siling labuyo, and coconut cream sauce are used to prepare this meal, which has a coconut cream foundation. There are more variations produced using crab, various fish, and shark flesh.

Pancit Bato

Bicol has its unique Pancit dish, like other regions in the Philippines. The noodles in Pancit Bato, which come from the Camarines Sur town of Bato, are really a little bit thicker and harder in texture.

Kandingga

Bopis, which is widely known in Bicol as Kandingga, is prepared by the Bicolanos using parts of pig, including the heart, lungs, and spleen, which are boiled in coconut milk and vinegar.

Pili Nuts

The Pili, an indigenous nut that is largely grown in the Bicol area, is another reason for Bicol pride. While you may eat pili nuts raw, which have a soft yet crunchy taste, their tastes also change when they are either roasted or caramelized.

Nilupak

This local Bicol treat is a creamy kind of kakanin, prepared from cooked kamoteng kahoy (cassava) or banana that has been mashed into a pulp and combined with sugar, condensed milk, and shredded coconut.

Puto Bukayo

Most Filipinos are familiar with this spherical, chewy puto, which is stuffed with sweet, extra-crunchy coconut that has been fried in brown sugar. The preponderance of Bicolanos favor this sweet delicacy because of the puto's softness and the fried coconut filling.

Biniribid

This delicacy's primary ingredients are coconut milk and rice flour, just like the majority of Bicolano food staples. It is deep-fried and either covered in a brown sugar glaze or a white sugar glaze.

Sili Ice Cream

Sili Ice cream's velvety, frozen texture is broken up by a dash of spiciness from the tastes of chile and coconut milk. If the spiciness worries you, you can select from four levels of spiciness based on your tolerance for heat.
